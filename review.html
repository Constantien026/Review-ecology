What is Ecology?

Ecology is a branch of biology that deals with the relations of organisms to one another and to their physical surroundings. It examines how life at every scale — from a single organism to the entire biosphere — interacts with living and non-living components of the environment.

Big Questions Ecology Asks

  • How is the "Balance of Nature" maintained?
  • How does human activity disrupt, restore, and maintain the balance of nature?
  • How are we responding to the call for environmental protection?
  • Why is the stability of ecosystems worldwide so urgent?

Key Ecological Terms

Organism
An individual animal, plant, or single-celled life form. The most basic unit studied in ecology.
Population
All individuals of the same species living in a particular area or region at the same time.
Community
A group of interdependent organisms of different species growing or living together in a specified habitat.
Ecosystem
A biological community of interacting organisms AND their physical (abiotic) environment together.
Biome
A large naturally occurring community of flora and fauna occupying a major habitat — e.g., tropical forest or tundra.
Biosphere
The global sum of all ecosystems on Earth — the zone of life extending from ocean depths to the lower atmosphere.

Concepts of the Ecosystem

Biotic Community

Different species live together and interact in a given territory. Together, they form a biotic community — a biological organization built on relationships between living things.

Members of a biotic community interact not only among themselves, but also with their physical environment — and this combined system is an ecosystem.

Biotic Components (Living)

Autotrophs (Producers)
Make their own food using light energy (photosynthetic) or chemical energy (chemosynthetic). Example: plants, algae.
Heterotrophs (Consumers)
Cannot make their own food; consume other organisms. Divided into macroconsumers (larger animals) and microconsumers (microbes/decomposers).
Herbivores
Heterotrophs that feed solely on plants (producers). Example: cows, deer.
Carnivores
Heterotrophs that feed solely on other animals (consumers). Example: lions, sharks.
Omnivores
Heterotrophs that feed on both plants and animals. Example: bears, humans.
Saprotrophs / Decomposers
Absorb dissolved organic materials from dead matter and return nutrients to the environment. Example: bacteria, fungi.
Scavengers
Consume dead or decaying animals, bridging consumers and decomposers.

Abiotic Components (Non-living)

☀️ Climate: Light, Temperature, Moisture 🪨 Soil: Nutrients, Moisture, Acidity ⛰️ Topography: Altitude, Slope angle, Orientation

These abiotic factors shape where organisms can live and how they survive. They interact continuously with the biotic components of the ecosystem.

Types of Ecosystems

Natural Ecosystem
Formed without human intervention. Examples: forests, oceans, wetlands, grasslands. Generally more biodiverse and self-sustaining.
Man-made Ecosystem
Created or heavily altered by humans. Examples: farms, fish ponds, gardens, cities. Usually less stable and biodiversity is lower.

Ecological Balance & Food Relationships

Why Food Matters

One of the most basic relationships in a biotic community concerns food. Food serves two purposes for organisms: (1) as a source of energy, and (2) as building material for the body.

Food Chain

The simplest flow of energy: an organism feeds on another and is in turn fed upon by another — forming a linear sequence.

Plants (Producer) → Grasshopper (1° Consumer) → Frog (2° Consumer) → Snake (3° Consumer)

Food Web

In nature, organisms feed on multiple species and are fed upon by multiple species, creating a complex, interlocking network. The more species involved in a food web, the more stable the ecosystem is — greater biodiversity equals greater stability.

Trophic Levels & Energy Flow

Energy flows through a trophic structure: from producers (1st trophic level) → 1st-order consumers → higher-level consumers. Energy diminishes at each level — approximately 90% is lost as heat at every step (only ~10% is passed on).

What Limits Population Growth?

  • Biotic limiting factors: Predators and consumers control the growth of prey populations.
  • Abiotic limiting factors: Space, light, water, and nutrients restrict how large a population can grow.

Balance of Nature / Ecological Balance

A dynamic equilibrium in the population sizes of different species that results from interactions among members of a biotic community and their physical environment. It is maintained by the interdependence of different species populations — based on three basic needs:

🍃 Food 🛡️ Protection 🔁 Reproduction

In a truly balanced ecosystem, all normal ecological processes continue unhampered.

Biogeochemical Cycles

A biogeochemical cycle is the pathway by which a chemical element or molecule moves through both biotic (living) and abiotic (non-living) compartments of the Earth. The major parts of the biosphere are connected by the flow of chemical elements and compounds. Matter from Earth's interior is released by volcanoes, while the atmosphere rapidly exchanges compounds with the biota and oceans. Exchanges between rocks, soils, and oceans are generally slower.

These cycles are essential for maintaining ecological balance — they ensure that nutrients are recycled and remain available to living organisms.

🌿

Carbon / CO₂ Cycle

The foundation of all organic life

▼

Carbon is the backbone of all organic molecules. It cycles continuously between the atmosphere, living organisms, oceans, and the Earth's crust. The primary driver of the carbon cycle is photosynthesis (which removes CO₂ from the atmosphere) and respiration (which returns it).

1
Photosynthesis: Plants, algae, and cyanobacteria absorb atmospheric CO₂ and convert it into glucose using sunlight energy. Carbon becomes stored in plant tissues.
2
Consumption: Herbivores eat plants, incorporating plant carbon into their own bodies. Carnivores then eat herbivores, passing carbon up the food chain.
3
Cellular Respiration: All living organisms (plants, animals, fungi, bacteria) break down glucose, releasing CO₂ back into the atmosphere.
4
Decomposition: When organisms die, decomposers (bacteria and fungi) break down organic matter, releasing CO₂ and methane (CH₄) back into the atmosphere.
5
Ocean Exchange: The ocean absorbs enormous amounts of CO₂ from the atmosphere; marine organisms use it to build calcium carbonate shells and skeletons.
6
Fossilization & Combustion: Over millions of years, dead organisms become fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas). Burning them releases ancient stored carbon back into the atmosphere rapidly.

⚠️ Human Impact

Burning fossil fuels and deforestation have significantly increased atmospheric CO₂ concentrations, disrupting the natural balance and intensifying the greenhouse effect and global warming.

⚗️

Nitrogen Cycle

Essential for proteins, DNA, and RNA

▼

Nitrogen makes up about 78% of the atmosphere (as N₂ gas), but most organisms cannot use it directly. It must be "fixed" — converted into usable forms like ammonia (NH₃) or nitrate (NO₃⁻) — before entering food chains. Nitrogen is essential for building amino acids, proteins, and nucleic acids (DNA/RNA).

1
Nitrogen Fixation: Specialized bacteria (e.g., Rhizobium in root nodules of legumes, and free-living Azotobacter) convert atmospheric N₂ into ammonia (NH₃). Lightning can also fix small amounts of nitrogen.
2
Nitrification: Nitrifying bacteria (e.g., Nitrosomonas, Nitrobacter) convert ammonia → nitrite (NO₂⁻) → nitrate (NO₃⁻) in the soil. Nitrates can be absorbed by plant roots.
3
Assimilation: Plants absorb nitrates through roots and build them into amino acids and proteins. Animals obtain nitrogen by consuming plants or other animals.
4
Ammonification: When organisms die or excrete waste, decomposers (bacteria, fungi) break down organic nitrogen compounds back into ammonia, returning it to the soil.
5
Denitrification: Denitrifying bacteria (e.g., Pseudomonas) convert nitrates back into N₂ or N₂O gas, releasing it into the atmosphere and completing the cycle.

⚠️ Human Impact

Synthetic fertilizers introduce excess nitrogen into ecosystems, causing eutrophication in water bodies — algal blooms that deplete oxygen and kill aquatic life.

💧

Water (Hydrological) Cycle

The continuous movement of water through Earth's systems

▼

Water is essential for all known life and is constantly moving through the biosphere. The water cycle connects the atmosphere, land surfaces, oceans, and living organisms. It also transports heat and dissolved nutrients across ecosystems.

1
Evaporation: Solar energy heats surface water in oceans, lakes, and rivers, converting liquid water into water vapor that rises into the atmosphere.
2
Transpiration: Plants release water vapor through tiny pores (stomata) in their leaves. In forests, this accounts for a large proportion of atmospheric moisture.
3
Condensation: Water vapor rises, cools, and condenses around dust particles to form clouds and fog. Water droplets cluster together into clouds.
4
Precipitation: Water falls back to Earth's surface as rain, snow, sleet, or hail, depending on atmospheric temperature.
5
Surface Runoff & Infiltration: Water flows over land into rivers and streams (runoff), or seeps into the ground to become groundwater and recharge aquifers (infiltration).
6
Absorption by Organisms: Living organisms take in water through roots, drinking, or absorption through skin, using it for metabolic processes before releasing it back through respiration and excretion.

⚠️ Human Impact

Deforestation reduces transpiration, altering rainfall patterns. Urbanization creates impervious surfaces, increasing runoff and reducing groundwater recharge. Climate change intensifies the entire cycle.

⛰️

Phosphorus Cycle

A slow cycle with no atmospheric phase

▼

Phosphorus is critical for building DNA, RNA, ATP (energy currency of cells), and cell membranes. Unlike carbon and nitrogen, phosphorus has no gaseous phase — it cycles slowly through rocks, soil, water, and living organisms. It is often the limiting nutrient in many ecosystems.

1
Weathering of Rocks: Phosphorus is slowly released from phosphate-containing rocks (like apatite) through weathering by rain, wind, and acidic compounds over long geological time scales.
2
Soil Absorption: Released phosphate ions (PO₄³⁻) dissolve in soil water and can be absorbed directly by plant roots. Mycorrhizal fungi greatly enhance plant phosphorus uptake.
3
Assimilation: Plants incorporate phosphorus into organic molecules. Animals obtain it by consuming plants or other animals. It is used to build bones, teeth, and energy molecules (ATP).
4
Decomposition: When organisms die, decomposers break down organic phosphorus compounds, returning inorganic phosphate to the soil for reuse by plants.
5
Runoff to Oceans: Phosphate washes into rivers and eventually the ocean, where it sinks to form sediments. Over millions of years, geological uplift brings it back to the surface.

⚠️ Human Impact

Mining phosphate rock for fertilizers and releasing phosphorus in sewage accelerates the cycle and causes freshwater and coastal eutrophication — destructive algal blooms that suffocate aquatic ecosystems.

🫧

Oxygen Cycle

Closely linked to the carbon and water cycles

▼

Oxygen makes up about 21% of Earth's atmosphere and is essential for aerobic respiration. The oxygen cycle is deeply intertwined with the carbon cycle — photosynthesis produces oxygen while consuming CO₂, and respiration does the opposite. It also involves the water cycle, as water molecules are split during photosynthesis.

1
Photosynthesis (O₂ Production): Producers (plants, algae, cyanobacteria) split water molecules (H₂O) using sunlight, releasing oxygen (O₂) as a byproduct into the atmosphere.
2
Aerobic Respiration (O₂ Consumption): Nearly all organisms (plants, animals, fungi, most bacteria) consume oxygen to break down glucose, releasing CO₂ and water as byproducts.
3
Decomposition: Aerobic decomposers use oxygen to break down dead organic matter, returning CO₂, water, and mineral nutrients to the environment.
4
Ozone Layer Formation: Some O₂ in the upper atmosphere is converted to ozone (O₃) by UV radiation. The ozone layer shields Earth's surface from harmful ultraviolet radiation.
5
Combustion & Oxidation: Burning of organic matter (forest fires, fossil fuels) and rusting of metals consumes O₂, releasing CO₂ and oxides back into the environment.

⚠️ Human Impact

Burning fossil fuels, deforestation, and industrial pollution deplete atmospheric oxygen (slowly) and damage the ozone layer (CFCs). Deoxygenation of water bodies ("dead zones") is a growing problem in many regions.

📖 Study Q & A

Q What is the difference between a food chain and a food web? ▾
A food chain is the simplest representation of energy flow — a linear sequence where organism A eats organism B, which is eaten by organism C. A food web is more realistic: it shows the complex, interlocking feeding relationships where each organism may feed on multiple species and be fed upon by multiple species. Food webs are far more stable than food chains — if one species disappears in a web, the ecosystem can compensate through other pathways.
Q Why does energy decrease as it moves up trophic levels? ▾
At each trophic level, organisms use most of the energy they consume for their own metabolic processes — growth, movement, reproduction, and heat production. Only about 10% of energy is stored in body tissues that can be passed on to the next level. This is why food chains rarely have more than 4–5 levels: there simply isn't enough energy left to support higher-level consumers beyond that point. This principle is represented by the Ecological Pyramid.
Q How does greater biodiversity contribute to ecosystem stability? ▾
Greater biodiversity creates a more complex and interconnected food web. When one species declines or disappears, other species can fill similar ecological roles (redundancy). This means the ecosystem is less vulnerable to collapse from the loss of any single species. A more biodiverse system also has more pathways for energy flow and nutrient cycling, making it more resilient to disturbances like drought, disease, or invasive species.
Q What is the role of decomposers in an ecosystem? ▾
Decomposers (mainly bacteria and fungi) are critical for breaking down dead organic matter — the remains of plants and animals. This decomposition releases nutrients (like nitrogen and phosphorus) that are locked in dead tissue back into the soil and water, where they can be reused by producers. Without decomposers, nutrients would remain locked in dead matter, cycling would halt, and ecosystems would collapse. They are the critical link that "closes" all biogeochemical cycles.
Q Why is nitrogen fixation so important? ▾
Although nitrogen gas (N₂) makes up 78% of the atmosphere, most organisms cannot use it directly. Nitrogen fixation — the conversion of N₂ into ammonia (NH₃) or nitrates (NO₃⁻) by specialized bacteria — makes nitrogen available to plants and, through them, to all other organisms. Without nitrogen fixation, there would be no way to replenish soil nitrogen used by plants, and the synthesis of proteins, DNA, and RNA would be severely limited, preventing life as we know it from existing.
Q What makes the phosphorus cycle unique compared to carbon and nitrogen cycles? ▾
The phosphorus cycle is unique because it has no significant atmospheric phase. Carbon and nitrogen both cycle through the atmosphere as gases (CO₂, N₂, N₂O), allowing for relatively rapid global redistribution. Phosphorus, however, moves only through water, soil, rocks, and living organisms. The cycle is extremely slow — the geological phase (from ocean sediments back to surface rock via tectonic uplift) takes millions of years. This makes phosphorus the most common limiting nutrient in ecosystems.
Q What are abiotic factors and how do they affect organisms? ▾
Abiotic factors are the non-living physical and chemical components of an environment. The main categories are: Climate (light intensity, temperature, rainfall, humidity), Soil (nutrient content, pH, moisture, texture), and Topography (altitude, slope angle, orientation). These factors determine which organisms can survive in a given area — for example, low light limits plant growth, extreme temperatures restrict the range of many animals, and soil pH affects what nutrients plants can absorb. Abiotic factors directly set the carrying capacity of an ecosystem.
Q How do human activities disrupt the balance of nature? ▾
Humans disrupt ecological balance in multiple ways: Deforestation removes habitats and disrupts the carbon and water cycles. Burning fossil fuels rapidly releases ancient carbon, elevating CO₂ and causing climate change. Overhunting/overfishing removes key species from food webs, causing trophic cascades. Agricultural fertilizers introduce excess nitrogen and phosphorus into waterways (eutrophication). Pollution introduces toxins that bioaccumulate up food chains. Invasive species introduced by humans outcompete native species and collapse local food webs.
